The Situation

A new product, a broken process, and three months to launch.

Private Credit was a new line of business at Moody's, announced in late 2025. Analysts were evaluating complex, compliance-driven deals through spreadsheet-heavy manual workflows, interpreting financial rules by hand with no consistent structure.

I joined as lead designer with the deadline three months out and no formal handoff from the previous designer. What I walked into had five compounding problems.

01

No established business process

How Private Credit would actually operate was still being figured out while we built the tool. There was no stable foundation to design against.

02

Requirements getting lost

Analysts told process, who told product, who told design. By the time requirements reached me they were misrepresented or missing entirely.

03

Analysts losing confidence

The people meant to use the tool couldn't see their feedback in the designs, and started questioning whether working with the team was worth their time.

04

Design built on assumptions

The existing tool was designed without direct analyst input. Core workflows weren't supported, and the team had no way to find out.

05

A hard deadline with no runway

Three months to launch, no context from the previous designer. Every day untangling the situation was a day not spent building.

Plan of Action

Three things had to happen in parallel.

I quickly assessed what was blocking us and focused on three tracks at once: understanding what analysts actually needed, rebuilding their confidence through visibility, and fixing how information flowed between teams.

01

Identify immediate need

Analysts were clear the existing design didn't reflect what they asked for. Before anything else, I needed to understand what they actually needed.

02

Rebuild analyst confidence

The analysts had started questioning whether collaborating with the product team was worth their time. Getting that trust back was a prerequisite to getting anything done.

03

Fix how information moved

Requirements were getting distorted across too many layers. I needed a more direct way to gather and align on what needed to be built.

Research

I sat in with analysts, product, and process separately, then together.

To understand what each team was actually saying, I joined their meetings individually before trying to align them. I spent the first two weeks taking notes and mapping where requirements were getting lost. Three layers of communication meant that by the time something reached me, it had often been reinterpreted or dropped entirely.

It quickly became clear that analysts needed a way to upload documents and create covenants for each facility they were monitoring. Neither was supported in the existing tool, despite both having been raised multiple times.

I did a gap analysis of the existing design to map exactly what was missing. The findings were concrete: no way to monitor covenants, no separation between global and periodic data, no way to assign individual due dates, no way to organize uploaded documents.

Gap analysis of the existing design, annotated with what was missing and what had to be added for launch.

The harder work was upstream of the UI: the deal logic itself. I mapped how analysts matched entities and applied rating rules, turning tangled, case-by-case judgment into structured decision flows the tool could actually support.

LP mapping logic. Where the system auto-validates an entity and where the analyst steps in, mapped as one consistent flow.
Rating logic for a new facility. The rules behind a rating, structured into a repeatable decision path after methodology changes.

The core insight: Analysts didn't need more inputs. They needed structured decision-making. The tool should guide them toward consistent, defensible evaluations, not just capture data and leave the interpretation to them.

Design

Rather than designing in isolation, I brought analysts into the process directly.

Given how much had been lost in translation before I joined, I wanted analysts to see their requirements reflected in designs as they evolved, not after decisions had already been made. I ran two collaborative design sessions, walking through early work together and pressure-testing assumptions in real time.

Ideating in Figma with analyst champions. Designs evolved in real time, with feedback captured directly on the canvas.

The bigger shift was conceptual. Instead of a form that captured data and left the hard thinking to the analyst, the tool became a structured system that guided the decision.

Before

Raw data and manual calc

Analysts collected facility data, uploaded spreadsheets, and interpreted financial rules by hand in ad-hoc formulas and free-text notes. Every deal was structured a little differently.

After two sessions, we reached sign-off on the design direction. The key additions:

Covenant Tracking

The most critical missing workflow: letting analysts track the terms of each deal. Added as a launch feature.

Global vs Periodic Data

A clear split between data that stays fixed and data that changes each reporting period.

Document Upload

Sorted document upload with AI-assisted data extraction, so nothing lives in scattered spreadsheets.

I annotated the direction so every change traced back to a specific gap analysts had raised. Nothing shipped that wasn't answering a documented need.

Changing How We Worked with AI

Fixing the product wasn't enough. I changed how the team worked, and how the work got built.

The process

In parallel with the design work, I recognized that the communication breakdown was structural, not a one-off fix. Requirements would keep getting distorted unless the process changed. I drafted a proposal for a new, more direct way of collaborating across design, product, process, and the analyst team.

The anchor of the new model: A standing weekly design session that analyst champions were required to attend. Requirements were defined, designs evolved in real time, and decisions were made on the spot rather than relayed through layers of communication.

The tooling

The deadline also changed how I worked as a designer. To move fast without losing rigor, I prototyped with AI-assisted tools and took designs straight into code, shortening the loop from idea to shippable UI.

Results

Launched on time, to real users, with real adoption.

The Private Credit tool launched on time. Given where we started, that was the most meaningful measure of success. Three months after launch, in a presentation to senior leadership, the team reported adoption numbers that validated both the design decisions and the new way of working.

Looking back: I'd push harder to get direct analyst access earlier. The moments we got the most useful signal were always when we went straight to end users.

What's next: pushing further into AI-assisted structuring of inputs, faster prototyping, and code-driven design workflows, so the tool keeps guiding decisions as Private Credit scales.
