The Challenge

A global bank. Seven tools. No way to see what mattered.

Everything ran through Outlook, Excel, and a handful of internal utilities. People kept the whole operation moving by memory and email, switching between systems dozens of times a day.

01

Daily work was spread across seven disconnected systems, with no single source of truth. People switched between them dozens of times a day.

02

Urgent work got buried

Urgent, regulated tasks sat in the same list as everyday busywork. The easy work got done; the important work slipped.

No oversight, duplicated effort

A live compliance risk

My Role

One of four designers. I owned the part that made it a hub.

Each designer led their own tools; I was primary on two. But the piece that turned seven tools into one product, the shared dashboard every role depended on, was mine. It was the hardest problem on the project.

The unifying dashboard

I designed the one screen every role shared. It went through more iterations than anything else we built, because it had to make sense of all seven streams at once.

The research engine

I facilitated the workshops and ran the interviews across three time zones, including early-morning calls to reach the team in India.

The bridge to development

I was the daily link to the engineers, building the relationship with the lead developer and writing the specs that kept the build true to the design.

Two primary work streams

I led the end-to-end design of two of the seven tools, with a second designer supporting to validate and pressure-test the work.

Plan of Action

With no precedent to lean on, I focused on three things at once.

With a live deadline, I couldn't research, then design, then test in order. I ran all three at once, so every decision was grounded in a real need and tested before it shipped.

Map how work really flowed

Before combining anything, I needed to see how work actually moved across the seven tools and four regions. Guessing is what had sunk similar projects before.

Give each role its own view

A junior analyst and a regional manager need to see completely different things. I built the system around each person's priorities from the very first wireframe.

Design for adoption, not just launch

A tool no one uses solves nothing. So I treated the rollout, training, and how we would measure success as part of the design from day one.

Research

Before designing anything, we watched how people worked. One finding changed everything.

We started by watching people work and asking them to talk through what they were doing. I ran junior and senior sessions separately on purpose: juniors went quiet in front of the VPs, and they were closest to where the work actually broke.

Swimlane mapping exposed overlapping workflows and bottlenecks across the seven tools. Each region had quietly invented its own workarounds to cope.

The finding that changed everything: the problem was never too many tools. It was that nothing showed people what to do first. Urgent work kept losing to whatever was easiest to close, so the hub had to make priority obvious.

How I prioritized under a live deadline: regulatory risk broke the ties. Anything compliance-related, like secure document upload, shipped first. Nice-to-haves waited. Here, the wrong cut isn't a worse experience, it's a breach.

Design

Nothing to inherit. So the real decision was one view, or seven.

There was no existing product to evolve and no pattern to borrow. With a live deadline, the call that shaped everything was this: give each of the seven streams its own tailored screen, or design one shared view that worked across all of them. Seven neat screens would have just moved the fragmentation into a single window. So I designed one role-aware dashboard, and made surfacing priority its whole job.

That's the finished product. Getting there wasn't smooth, though. Partway through, the bank moved every team onto a new design system, and we had to rebuild what we'd already shipped.

Before. Our first build, on AG Grid, a framework made for dense data tables.
After. The same screen, rebuilt in Material Design: one clean language for every tool.

What it led to: we were one of the first teams onto Material Design, so the patterns we worked out helped shape how the rest of the company adopted it.

Under all of it, the data is structured so AI-driven prioritization can layer on next, turning today's manual triage into tomorrow's automatic one.

Results

Shipped on time. Adopted fast. Measurable from day one.

Shipping wasn't the finish line, adoption was. I paired the launch with a global training program, manuals, live sessions, 1:1 coaching, and an online Q&A channel, then tracked adoption and time savings on dashboards shared with leadership.

The trade-off: I rolled the hub out one region at a time instead of all at once. It took longer, but every team got the support they needed to fully switch over.

The Work Manager Hub is now a core tool used daily across global teams. It became the single source of truth, paving the way for AI-driven predictive tasking in future releases.

Reflection

What I'd carry into the next one. And what I'd do sooner.

My biggest lesson wasn't about layout. It was learning to frame findings as business risks. "Urgent work gets buried" is easy to agree with and move past. "We're missing regulatory cutoffs and sending confidential files over email" gets a bank to act. Same finding, framed as the risk it actually was.

What I'd do sooner: bring developers in on day one. We involved them early, but the first handoffs still came back looking nothing like the designs. That's what pushed me to treat engineers as co-authors, and why we built the specs, component docs, and dev workshops.

The rest still holds. Every call came from a user insight, not an opinion. The hub didn't just replace seven tools; it gave a thousand people the same answer every morning: what needs me first?
